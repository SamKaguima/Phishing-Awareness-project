How Phishing Occurs

Gathering Information

Phishers may gather personal information from websites, data leaks, social media or other places where users give out information. They can use this information to disguise themselves as an organization their victim may be a member of or a local authority in which their victim may live under. Additionally, phishers may use information they gather from social media to pose as a family member or friend of a specific victim.








Common Tactics

Phishers use three common tactics in order to trick their victims

  1. General Greetings
  2. Familiarity and Sense of Urgency
  3. Phony Links and Files

General Greetings and Statements

When phishers disguise themselves as large organizations, they will use general greetings. This is so they can send emails to various people without having to change any of the information or context within the email. It also helps them avoid promoting suspicion, as many organizations may also use general greetings, especially when it comes to giving rewards or giving out general information.

Familiarity and Sense of Urgency

Along with general greetings, phishers may also use familiarity to trick their victims. This includes posing as an organization, as mentioned above, or posing as a friend/family member. These organizations can range from popular organizations, like Amazon or a nonprofit, to the company a victim may currently or have previously worked at or even to a small local business. Phishers then create a sense of urgency in their emails in order to trick their victims into clicking strange links or performing strange tasks.

When posing as an organization, phishers use various tactics such as offering a reward with time a limit, threatening financial loss, or threats of legal consequences. Common methods of rewards with time limits are gift card opportunities with a 2 day time limit or a free vacation that can be claimed within the next week. Phishers may pose as their victim’s bank, making false claims about money loss, or a local police station, claiming they owe money for a ticket. Phishers may also use a local police station or governing body to claim a law violation and threaten legal action if certain actions aren’t taken. Both methods also tie into time limits as it can be assumed or explicitly stated that they only have a limited time before they face dreadful consequences.

A common method phishers use when posing as a friend or family member is claiming that they are in trouble and in need of money. This can range from claims of being stranded or kidnapped to simply needing a few dollars for a menial task. In these cases they may also attach a place to send money. Phishers may also send links, claiming they are for memberships or rewards.

Malicious Links and Files

In most cases, when phishers want to obtain personal information, they are most likely to send malicious links. These links tend to take users to malicious websites that seem legit, but only collect information from their victims. This includes login information for certain websites, banking information, and company information from employees. An example of this is the paypal phishing scam, where users are given links that take them to a website that looks exactly like the paypal login. Phishers may also send links for their victims to give them money or links that automatically download malicious software onto their victim’s computer.

Phishers can also download malicious software onto their victim’s computer via tricking them into downloading attached files. The files can look like regular files with normal contents in them, however, they secretly contain corrupt code that can be downloaded onto their victim’s computer and corrupt their systems. The files may contain code for ransomware, worms, viruses, Trojan horses, or spyware. All of these can be used to obtain information from files in the victim’s computer or from the user’s activity.








How to Spot and Prevent Phishing

Check the sender

Before clicking on an email, make sure to check who sent the email. This can be done by either hovering over the name of the sender or, if the user supposedly knows the sender, verifying with them on the origins of the email. Additionally, always be wary if a company or person is emailing for the first time unexpectedly. Some email sites may include warnings for potential scammers or malicious persons.

Look for Common Tactics

General Greetings

When it comes to personal information or an email that would normally be directed to a certain user, it is important to be wary when these emails include general statements and greetings. For example, a bank or an employer using statements like “Dear customer,” or “Dear employee,” instead of using a specific name.

Familiarity and Sense of Urgency

In order to avoid being phished via familiarity and a sense of urgency, make sure to keep calm and stay level headed. Verify with the sender by either going to the official website, contacting the sender through a verified method, or meeting the sender in person, if applicable and possible.

Check Links and Files

The best way to avoid getting scammed via malicious links and files is by never downloading files or clicking links. Understandably, this is not always avoidable. A good way to avoid malicious links is by hovering over the link and checking the address that appears on screen. If the address contains an unfamiliar or suspicious domain do not click the link. When it comes to files, be wary if the file is an executable file like .exe or .zip files. Additionally, antivirus software can be used to scan the file to ensure it is safe to open.

Check for Mistakes, Discontinuities and Mysterious Behaviour

In most cases, organizations are going to ensure their emails do not contain any grammar mistakes, especially within automated emails. This means that when emails that are supposedly from these organizations contain spelling or grammatical errors, it is most likely a phishing email.

Additionally, it is important to check for discontinuities within emails. This would include information within emails that the supposed sender wouldn’t know or say, such as their bank sending an email using a nickname from their Instagram account or a friend using terms or slang they have never used in person before. It can also include a company a potential victim has never heard of nor interacted with sending emails about rewards they earned.
